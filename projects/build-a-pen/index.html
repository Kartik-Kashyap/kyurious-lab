The illusion of “I could make this myself”

When you buy a ₹5 pen, it feels like a small, simple object. A tube of plastic, some ink, a little metal, done. In your head, you imagine a single factory, maybe a single worker on a line.

But the moment you try to make one from nothing, reality glitches. You crash into mines, refineries, labs, ports, trade laws, regulators, patents, and public outrage about plastic.

What looks like a “cheap pen” is actually a physical record of thousands of people solving problems you will never hear about:

  • engineers who figured out how to machine the tiny ball at the tip;
  • chemists who made sure the ink doesn’t fade, smear, or poison you;
  • miners who pull ore and oil out of dangerous places;
  • logistics teams that quietly move it all across continents;
  • bureaucrats who make sure it doesn’t explode, leak, or set your house on fire.

You never paid most of them directly. Some of the real cost is pushed onto future generations, onto the climate, onto overworked people you’ll never meet.

That doesn’t mean you should feel guilty for existing. It just means: these “small” objects are civilizational miracles. They deserve a tiny bit of reverence.

Use your things well. Break them slower. Lose them less. When the pen finally runs out, imagine all the invisible hands you just used up—and say a quiet, slightly dramatic “thank you.”
